Background reading

Methods for Solving PDEs

A survey of classical approaches — finite differences, finite elements, spectral and meshless methods, explicit and implicit time stepping — each illustrated on the same benchmark problem.

§ 1

Grid and mesh-based methods

The most widely used family of PDE solvers starts by laying a grid (or mesh) over the spatial domain and turning the continuous equation $\mathcal{L}_a\, u = f$ into a finite-dimensional linear system $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{f}$. The three main variants differ in how the discrete equations are derived.

1.1 Finite Difference Method (FDM)

FDM replaces each derivative with a difference quotient. On a uniform grid with spacing $h$, the centred approximation to the second derivative is

FDM stencil
$$u''(x_i) \;\approx\; \frac{u_{i-1} - 2u_i + u_{i+1}}{h^2}.$$

Applying this at every interior grid point gives the tridiagonal system $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{f}$ with $A_{ii} = 2/h^2$ and $A_{i,i\pm 1} = -1/h^2$. The main virtue of FDM is simplicity: it is the fastest method to implement and understand. Its main limitation is that it assumes a structured grid — handling irregular geometries or local refinement requires substantial extra machinery.

1.2 Finite Element Method (FEM)

Rather than approximating derivatives directly, FEM works with the variational (weak) form of the PDE. Multiplying $-u'' = f$ by a test function $v$ and integrating by parts gives

Weak form
$$\int_0^1 u'(x)\, v'(x)\, dx \;=\; \int_0^1 f(x)\, v(x)\, dx \qquad \forall\, v \text{ with } v(0)=v(1)=0.$$

The domain is divided into elements (intervals in 1D, triangles or tetrahedra in higher dimensions), and the solution is expanded as $u \approx \sum u_j\, \varphi_j$ where the $\varphi_j$ are piecewise-polynomial basis functions (hat functions for linear elements). Substituting into the weak form and choosing $v = \varphi_i$ yields the stiffness system

FEM system
$$\mathbf{K}\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{F}, \qquad K_{ij} = \int \varphi_i'\, \varphi_j'\, dx, \quad F_i = \int f\, \varphi_i\, dx.$$

On a uniform 1D grid with linear elements, $\mathbf{K}$ is the same tridiagonal matrix as in FDM (up to a factor of $h$). The payoff comes in higher dimensions: FEM handles complex geometries naturally through unstructured meshes and provides a rigorous error theory via the variational framework. It is the dominant method in structural mechanics and solid-body physics.

1.3 Finite Volume Method (FVM)

FVM integrates the PDE over small control volumes centred on each grid point. For $-u'' = f$ on a control volume $[x_{i-\frac12}, x_{i+\frac12}]$:

FVM flux balance
$$-\bigl[u'(x_{i+\frac12}) - u'(x_{i-\frac12})\bigr] \;=\; \int_{x_{i-\frac12}}^{x_{i+\frac12}} f\, dx \;\approx\; h\, f_i.$$

The face fluxes $u'(x_{i\pm\frac12})$ are approximated by centred differences, giving the same tridiagonal system as before. The conceptual difference is that FVM enforces conservation of the integrated quantity across cell faces — a property that makes it the preferred method in computational fluid dynamics, where mass, momentum, and energy must be conserved exactly at the discrete level.

A note on 1D
On a uniform grid in one dimension, FDM, FEM, and FVM all produce the same algebraic system. This is a well-known coincidence that breaks down in higher dimensions, on non-uniform meshes, or with higher-order elements. The interactive comparison below confirms this: the three solutions lie exactly on top of each other.
§ 2

Spectral methods

Instead of local basis functions, spectral methods expand the solution in global basis functions — typically Fourier sines/cosines or Chebyshev polynomials. For the Poisson equation with homogeneous Dirichlet conditions, the natural basis is $\{\sin(k\pi x)\}_{k=1}^\infty$, which are eigenfunctions of $-d^2/dx^2$:

Spectral solve
$$f(x) = \sum_k \hat{f}_k \sin(k\pi x) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad u(x) = \sum_k \frac{\hat{f}_k}{(k\pi)^2}\, \sin(k\pi x).$$

The algorithm is: (1) compute the sine coefficients $\hat{f}_k$ via a discrete sine transform, (2) divide each by $(k\pi)^2$, (3) transform back. No linear system is solved — only pointwise divisions in frequency space.

Spectral methods achieve exponential convergence for smooth problems: doubling the number of modes can add several digits of accuracy. The trade-off is that they require simple geometries (intervals, rectangles, spheres) and smooth solutions. § 3

Meshless methods

Meshless or particle-based methods bypass the mesh entirely. In radial basis function (RBF) collocation, the solution is represented as a sum of translated basis functions centred at scattered points $\{x_j\}$:

RBF ansatz
$$u(x) \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{M} \lambda_j\, \varphi\!\bigl(\lvert x - x_j\rvert;\, \varepsilon\bigr), \qquad \varphi(r;\varepsilon) = e^{-\varepsilon^2 r^2}.$$

The PDE is enforced at each collocation point: $-u''(x_i) = f(x_i)$ at interior points, $u(x_j) = 0$ at boundary points. This gives a dense linear system for the weights $\lambda_j$, in contrast to the sparse systems produced by grid-based methods.

The shape parameter $\varepsilon$ controls the width of each basis function. Small $\varepsilon$ (wide, flat) gives high accuracy but poor conditioning; large $\varepsilon$ (narrow, peaked) is stable but less accurate. This accuracy–stability trade-off is a central challenge in RBF methods. Techniques such as RBF-QR and RBF-GA partially mitigate it.

Other meshless approaches include Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH), widely used for free-surface flows and astrophysics, and the Element-Free Galerkin method, which combines meshless interpolation with the variational framework of FEM.

§ 4

Time integration

For time-dependent PDEs such as the heat equation $u_t = u_{xx}$, the spatial discretisation (by any of the methods above) turns the PDE into a system of ODEs $\mathbf{u}'(t) = \mathbf{A}\,\mathbf{u}(t)$. The remaining question is how to march forward in time.

4.1 Explicit schemes

The simplest option is Forward Euler: advance each time step using only known values from the current step.

Forward Euler
$$u_i^{n+1} = u_i^n + \frac{\Delta t}{h^2}\bigl(u_{i-1}^n - 2u_i^n + u_{i+1}^n\bigr).$$

No linear system is solved — just a pointwise update. But stability imposes the CFL condition $\Delta t \le h^2/2$: the time step must be small relative to the grid spacing squared. Violating this limit causes the numerical solution to blow up exponentially. Higher-order explicit schemes (Runge–Kutta) improve accuracy but are subject to the same constraint.

4.2 Implicit schemes

Backward Euler evaluates the spatial operator at the new time level:

Backward Euler
$$u_i^{n+1} - \frac{\Delta t}{h^2}\bigl(u_{i-1}^{n+1} - 2u_i^{n+1} + u_{i+1}^{n+1}\bigr) = u_i^n.$$

This requires solving a tridiagonal system at every time step, but the pay-off is unconditional stability: $\Delta t$ can be made as large as accuracy permits, with no blow-up. The Crank–Nicolson scheme averages the explicit and implicit operators, giving second-order accuracy in time while remaining unconditionally stable.
